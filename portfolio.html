Background

I learn the whole system.
Then I fix it.

There's a pattern in every project I've worked on: the solution was hiding at the boundary between two disciplines that weren't in conversation with each other. The color failure in a colonoscopy camera wasn't purely an optics problem — it was a sensor physics problem expressed through software. The yield failure in manufacturing wasn't a production problem — it was a precision engineering problem embedded in an assembly workflow. The difference between finding those connections and missing them is whether you actually understand all the systems involved.

I'm genuinely autodidactic. At GI View, I taught myself optics and electronics from scratch while managing three concurrent FDA programs — not because the job required it, but because I needed to understand how things actually worked. I kept studying beyond what any role demanded. The same has been true for signal processing, regulatory science, CAD, image processing algorithms. Each domain becomes a tool for seeing the connections the previous domains were missing.

What energizes me most is full ownership of a domain — defining quality criteria, setting the path independently, making the calls. The camera system at GI View was the clearest example: complete ownership of a complex system crossing optics, electronics, software, and manufacturing, with the freedom to decide what success looked like. That combination of autonomy, cross-disciplinary depth, and a hard problem is where I do my best work.

I've also been a natural coordinator on every team I've been part of. At GI View I managed four external engineering consultants across optics, software, mechanical, and algorithms — while keeping three FDA programs aligned, mentoring junior engineers, and maintaining vendor relationships. That leadership has always come naturally. Building on it formally is the next step.

I came to the US for my MS in Bioengineering at Northeastern for the same reason: go deeper, learn more rigorously, so the next thing I build is better than the last. I believe biotech needs product managers who've been in the lab — who understand what an engineer means when they say "that's not possible," and know the difference between a real constraint and a framing problem.

Philosophy

A different way of thinking
about product in biotech.

Most product frameworks weren't designed for regulated environments. Here's what I've learned building medical devices from the ground up.

01
Every design decision has a regulatory shadow.
In medical devices, the FDA pathway isn't something you engage at the end — it's embedded in every early architecture choice. I've watched teams choose technically elegant solutions that added 18 months to their timeline because they didn't account for the submission pathway. The most important product question at a startup isn't just "what does the user want?" It's "what does the user want, what will the FDA accept, what can we actually build — and in what order?"
02
The breakthrough lives between the disciplines.
The problems worth solving in biotech don't live cleanly inside one field. Our colonoscopy cameras failed because no one was holding the optical physics, the firmware constraints, and the FDA submission pathway in their head at the same time. When I taught myself color science to fix the visualization system, or integrated mechanical tooling with real-time software to fix our assembly line, the solutions came from refusing to treat those domains as separate. The most useful thing I can do in a room is see where the disciplines touch — and work there.
03
Own the problem, not just your piece of it.
The colonoscopy device was in crisis months before FDA submission. The codebase was a black box, the previous engineer couldn't explain his own work, and no one else was positioned to fix it. I didn't ask whose responsibility it was. I reverse-engineered the full system, rebuilt the calibration from scratch, and turned around a failing vendor relationship while doing it. The habit isn't heroism — it's a conviction that the quality of the outcome matters more than the tidiness of the org chart. If the problem is real, it's mine.

March 2026
The PM who can't read a datasheet

In most industries, a PM who can't build the thing they're managing is fine. You don't need to know how to write React to make a good SaaS product.

In biotech and medical devices, I'm not sure that's true. The product and the science are the same thing. When you're designing a colonoscopy camera, the optics aren't a feature — they're the mechanism of action. The firmware isn't infrastructure — it determines what a physician sees during a procedure and whether they can detect an abnormality. The FDA submission isn't a compliance exercise — it's the primary product milestone.

A PM who can't engage with those layers technically isn't directing the product — they're describing it. The engineers are making the decisions, and the PM is translating after the fact.

The best PM I know how to be is one who understands the thing well enough to have an opinion about the tradeoffs — and earns the credibility to be in the room when those tradeoffs are made.

February 2026
What I learned watching endoscopy procedures

The first time I watched a colonoscopy procedure, I came with a list of product questions. What features do you use most? What's missing? What would make this better?

What I noticed was something else: the physician barely looked at the feature panel. Three years of engineering debate about which functions to include — and in the actual procedure, the camera moved and the physician looked.

What actually mattered was field of view. Color fidelity. The speed at which the image updated when the camera changed direction. None of those were on my list.

The gap between what users say they want and what they actually need, in a clinical context, is enormous — and dangerous. A wrong assumption in consumer tech costs you engagement metrics. A wrong assumption in medical devices costs you a clinical trial, an FDA submission, or worse, a patient outcome.

Getting in the room — literally into the operating room — is the only way I know to close that gap. Everything else is a model of reality. The procedure room is reality.

January 2026
The regulatory shadow

Every design decision in medical device development has a regulatory shadow.

Choose the wrong tissue enhancement approach and you've added a hardware component that needs its own FDA validation pathway — adding 12–18 months and a significant cost burden. Choose a software-based solution and you've opened a faster submission pathway but a harder engineering problem.

The best technical solution and the best regulatory strategy are rarely the same thing. And the PM is the person who has to reconcile them — before anyone writes a single line of code.
