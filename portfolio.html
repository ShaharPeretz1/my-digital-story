Background

I speak engineer.
I think product.
I deliver both.

I'm drawn to problems that don't fit neatly into one box. The kind that require you to hold multiple systems at once, understand how they interact, and find the path that only becomes visible when you see the whole picture. That's where I feel most alive - professionally and personally.

Outside of work, I hike steep mountains and get into ice baths. Not to suffer, but because there's something clarifying about a challenge that demands everything you have and gives you something real on the other side. I've found that the same is true for hard problems at work. The difficulty isn't a drawback — it's the point. It's what makes the solution worth building.

In medical devices, that disposition turns out to be exactly what product management requires. The best PM decisions — what to build, what to cut, what's a real constraint versus a framing problem — depend on understanding the full system. Not as a specialist in each, but deeply enough to ask the right questions, earn the trust of the people who are, and know when someone's telling you something can't be done versus something they haven't figured out yet.

I came up through R&D and engineering, which means I've been on the other side of the table. I know what it feels like when a PM doesn't understand what they're asking for — and I know how much faster things move when they do.

I'm at my best when I own something end to end. When I can define what success looks like and build toward it systematically. That's the kind of work I'm here to do.

Philosophy

A different way of thinking
about product in biotech.

In biotech, product decisions have consequences that most industries never have to think about. That changes how you need to think about the work. Here's what I've learned building medical devices from the ground up.

01
Technical depth is a product skill.
Technical depth isn't a bonus in biotech PM — it's the job. When you understand the science, you don't just follow the conversation — you shape it. You ask better questions, catch bad assumptions early, and size the effort realistically before it becomes a problem. The difference between a PM who understands the underlying system and one who doesn't isn't a matter of style. It shows up in every estimate, every tradeoff, every conversation with an engineer who's deciding whether to tell you the whole truth or the easy version.
02
The breakthrough lives between the disciplines.
The problems worth solving in biotech don't live cleanly inside one field. The real leverage sits at the intersection — where biology meets hardware, where engineering meets regulation, where what's technically possible meets what a clinician will actually use under pressure. That's where I've always found myself drawn to work. Not because the boundaries are interesting in the abstract, but because that's where the solution usually turns out to be hiding.
03
Own the problem, not just your piece of it.
Great products get built when someone decides the result matters more than the boundaries of their job description. I've always operated that way — not out of ambition, but because it's the only way to build something you're genuinely proud of. Full ownership means defining what success looks like, building toward it with clarity, and taking responsibility for what ships.

The result wasn't just a yield improvement. It was a repeatable, documented process where there hadn't been one before.

Tel Aviv University · Hardware · Algorithms
Validated
LiDAR Motion Capture — Wearable Assistive Device
Clinical biomechanical assessment typically requires dedicated lab equipment, trained technicians, and expensive motion capture systems. The goal here was to replace all of that with two iPhones and a belt.

I designed and built a complete wearable motion capture system using the LiDAR sensors built into iPhone Pro models — positioning them at the body's center of mass using a custom-designed adjustable belt harness I engineered for stability and comfort during movement. The system tracked sit-to-stand transitions in two axes simultaneously, capturing the full biomechanical signature of the movement.

The technical work spanned hardware and software end to end: calibrated the LiDAR sensors against an Instron testing machine as gold standard, developed custom MATLAB signal processing algorithms to extract movement sections from continuous recordings, and built computational models to quantify stability metrics — area under the curve, velocity, and acceleration of the center of mass. Validated across 12 test subjects and used the system to assess the effectiveness of an assistive mobility device in reducing fall risk during standing transitions.
Validated across 12 test subjects against Instron gold standard. Feasibility for clinical deployment established.

March 2026
The PM who can't read a datasheet

In most industries, a PM who can't build the thing they're managing is fine. You don't need to know how to write React to make a good SaaS product.

In biotech and medical devices, I'm not sure that's true. The product and the science are the same thing. When you're designing a colonoscopy camera, the optics aren't a feature — they're the mechanism of action. The firmware isn't infrastructure — it determines what a physician sees during a procedure and whether they can detect an abnormality. The FDA submission isn't a compliance exercise — it's the primary product milestone.

A PM who can't engage with those layers technically isn't directing the product — they're describing it. The engineers are making the decisions, and the PM is translating after the fact.

The best PM I know how to be is one who understands the thing well enough to have an opinion about the tradeoffs — and earns the credibility to be in the room when those tradeoffs are made.

February 2026
What I learned watching endoscopy procedures

The first time I watched a colonoscopy procedure, I came with a list of product questions. What features do you use most? What's missing? What would make this better?

What I noticed was something else: the physician barely looked at the feature panel. Three years of engineering debate about which functions to include — and in the actual procedure, the camera moved and the physician looked.

What actually mattered was field of view. Color fidelity. The speed at which the image updated when the camera changed direction. None of those were on my list.

The gap between what users say they want and what they actually need, in a clinical context, is enormous — and dangerous. A wrong assumption in consumer tech costs you engagement metrics. A wrong assumption in medical devices costs you a clinical trial, an FDA submission, or worse, a patient outcome.

Getting in the room — literally into the operating room — is the only way I know to close that gap. Everything else is a model of reality. The procedure room is reality.

January 2026
The regulatory shadow

Every design decision in medical device development has a regulatory shadow.

Choose the wrong tissue enhancement approach and you've added a hardware component that needs its own FDA validation pathway — adding 12–18 months and a significant cost burden. Choose a software-based solution and you've opened a faster submission pathway but a harder engineering problem.

The best technical solution and the best regulatory strategy are rarely the same thing. And the PM is the person who has to reconcile them — before anyone writes a single line of code.
